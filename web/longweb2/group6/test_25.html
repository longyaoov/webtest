    By Susan Miers Smith | ssmith@readingeagle.com | Reading Eagle and Susan Miers Smith
    UPDATED: August 19, 2021 at 2:40 AM EDT

    The scales, scutes and shells of reptiles may not be as pleasing to stroke as fur, for some people, but the creatures have their dedicated fans. Just stop by any pet store and you can find aisles of products devoted to reptile care and housing.

    Also, Animal Planet launched a show, “Scaled,” on Aug. 24. The series focuses on high-end reptile enclosures, similar to the network’s “Tanked” show about fish aquariums.

    Rodolfo “Rudy” Arceo, 42, says he has been a reptile enthusiast for more than 30 years. Earlier this summer, he took possession of a female ball python named Monty from the Animal Rescue League of Berks County. The 4-foot-long snake had been found slithering through an Amity Township neighborhood on July 5.

    “I’m keeping Monty as an example of what happens when you don’t monitor your cage,” Arceo said.

    Arceo said he was contacted by a relative of the original owner and was told that the pet snake had been loose for about six weeks.

    “She must have been in the house the majority of that time,” Arceo said, noting how good her condition was when he received her.

    When diabetic neuropathy forced him from his volunteer firefighting and onto disability a few years ago, he decided to spend his time doing reptile presentations and removing nuisance snakes from people’s yards. He launched Venom Institute Conservation & Education as a nonprofit and is still working on getting 501(c)(3) status from the Internal Revenue Service. Arceo, who lives in Centre Township, said he has help from about 10 volunteers spread out through Reading, Philadelphia and New York.

    Capturing attention

    Children in the Shoemakersville summer playground program were eager to get to meet some of Arceo’s pets during a Venom Institute presentation on July 19.

    There were no venomous critters on display, but he did bring an alligator, a 45-pound red-tailed boa, two ball pythons, a Brooks king snake, an Eastern rat snake, a bearded dragon, an African spurred tortoise, a snapping turtle and a spiny-tailed iguana.

    Arceo took the sleek black rat snake next to a tree, and it began to climb as he explained that the species like to dine on birds’ eggs in the wild.

    The children listened intently as Arceo talked about the unique requirements reptiles have in order to keep them as a pet: diet, lighting and heat. Those keys also vary between species.

    For example, he said, an African spurred tortoise, also called a sulcata tortoise, needs a diet of dark greens to supply calcium to make its top shell (carapace) and bottom shell (plastron) hard. The tortoises also enjoy daisies and hibiscus as a treat Arceo said.

    “Reptile metabolism is very slow,” Arceo said.

    He said that Monty the ball python eats one rat a week. On the other hand, Spinysaurus, a spiny-tailed iguana, eats mostly plant matter and insects and gets papaya and mango as a treat.

    Good first reptile

    Arceo recommended leopard geckos as a good first reptilian pet and suggested keeping it in at least a 10-gallon tank. They eat mostly mealworms and crickets.

    Arceo is a single father of three: daughter, Raven, 9, and sons Korbin, 7, and Takota, 5. All of them love reptiles, and Takota, who has Down syndrome, is the only one who does not have a pet of his own.

    Raven took all of the handler-friendly reptiles out into the audience during the Shoemakersville presentation. Her bearded dragon, Omar, was of particular interest to the crowd. Her beautiful tan and white piebald ball python, Snoopy, was tightly curled on top of her hand as she paraded him around.

    Attendees with permission slips were excited to line up after the talk and get a chance to hold or pet the reptiles.

    Up close and personal

    Some had a little trepidation as they lined up, asking if the creatures they were about to meet would bite them.

    “Anything with a mouth can bite,” Arceo advised.

    Arceo draped 45-pound red-tailed boa Ka around his shoulders, and Xander Valentine, 9, of Shoemakersville was among the first to pet the constrictor.

    Although he liked the snake, the tiny 6-month-old African spurred tortoise was Valentine’s favorite.

    Petting Ka was not good enough for Ethan Keppley, 8, of Hamburg. He had to wear the snake too.

    Many of the children wanted to meet Rampage, the young American alligator. She had her mouth taped shut for safety.

    “I didn’t know that an alligator could grow to be 14 or 15 feet long,” said Gabriella Valentino, 12, of Shoemakersville.

    Education is exactly what Arceo wants his audience to come away with after a presentation.
