Plan teed up to save Rainbow Springs golf courses

Lawmaker's bill aims to keep greens in operation

March 30, 2011
0 Comments
SHARE

By Lee Bergquist of the Journal Sentinel

March 30, 2011 0

A Republican legislator said Monday that she is drafting legislation to keep a pair of golf courses in operation at the former Rainbow Springs Resort, instead of returning the land to its natural state.

The bill is sure to come under fire from environmentalists and outdoors groups, many of whom applauded the state's $10.8 million acquisition in 2008.

At the time, the purchase of the 970-acre parcel - including more than 200 acres for the golf courses - was trumpeted for bringing new hunting and other recreational opportunities to southeastern Wisconsin.

The golf courses closed at the end of 2010. The plan for the land has been to use it for outdoor-based recreation, including hunting, hiking and fishing. The property is located along the Mukwonago River, which the Department of Natural Resources considers to be one of the most pristine in southern Wisconsin.

But Sen. Mary Lazich (R-New Berlin) said she's heard concerns since the state's purchase, including worries over the loss of the golf courses and a lack of input from the public at the time of the sale.

A March 10 meeting by the Department of Natural Resources in Mukwonago attracted 200 people. Lazich also held her own session with interested parties last week.

One major concern is the loss of the golf courses for use by the Mukwonago Area School District.

Andy Trudell, activities director and assistant principal of the high school, said Rainbow Springs' Par 3 course is used by the junior varsity golf team and a summer golf camp draws about 300 children. The regulation-size course is used by the school's varsity golf teams.

Other courses in the area, he said, would not tolerate so much playing pressure from students. Trudell estimated that changing courses would cost the school district about $3,500 a year.

State law currently prohibits the funds from the Knowles-Nelson Stewardship Fund - the state's largest purchase program - from buying land for golf courses.

Legislation would be needed to allow a private party to lease the course from the state and operate it as a viable business, Lazich said. The former assistant golf coach from the high school is working with others on plans to lease and manage the course, Trudell said.

Lazich said: "People have been utilizing this course for years and then all of a sudden, the state comes in and shuts off access. It seems unfair."

Lazich said that with so much acreage, the DNR could use the land for a variety of activities. The DNR's master planning process for the course, which will spell out those activities, has not started.

Former DNR Secretary George Meyer, executive director of the Wisconsin Wildlife Federation, said it was clear that when the state purchased the land, it was for hunting, fishing and hiking, not rounds of golf.

The plans for the low-lying golf course called for the land to revert to its natural wetland state.
