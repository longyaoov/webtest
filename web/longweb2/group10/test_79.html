        State officials break down state budget impasse, other unresolved issues at Evanston town hall meeting

        Leeks Lim/Daily Senior Staffer

        State Senator Daniel Biss addresses roughly 70 local residents at an Evanston town hall meeting Tuesday evening focused on the state budget impasse. Biss broke down the circumstances behind the impasse, explaining what has happened in Springfield since Gov. Bruce Rauner entered office.

        Fathma Rahman, Assistant Summer Editor
        June 15, 2016

        When state Rep. Laura Fine (D-Glenview) said she was “irked” by the media’s reporting that there is no compromise or collaboration in Springfield amid the budget impasse, some locals saw the issue in a new light at Tuesday’s town hall meeting.

        “I had a view that is defined by the press,” said Leo Sherman, a southern Evanston resident. “The press always breaks down the issue between Madigan and Rauner and how they can’t solve the budget, but there’s actually been a lot going on in terms of how the state has been working together and addressing these issues.”

        State Sen. Daniel Biss (D-Evanston), Rep. Robyn Gabel (D-Evanston) and Fine engaged in a discussion with roughly 70 Evanston residents at the meeting about the challenges legislators face in Springfield regarding the state budget impasse and other unresolved issues.

        Fine pointed to last summer, when she said legislators would go down to Springfield every week to meet and discuss the impasse. She said they also brought in experts to advise them as well as to get feedback on Gov. Bruce Rauner’s “turnaround agenda,” a plan to reshape Illinois by fixing the state budget and creating economic growth.

        Gabel spoke to some of the several consequences that have come from the state budget impasse, such as funding cuts to state colleges and universities which have led to rises in tuition and faculty layoffs. In addition, nearly 10,000 kids are no longer eligible for child care, co-pay across the state increased by 20 percent and there were major cuts to the public healthcare system, she said.

        “We all read the newspapers and the blogs and we get some information, but we wanted to make sure you get the whole picture,” Gabel said.

        Beginning the story on Dec. 31, 2014, about two weeks before Rauner took office, Biss outlined how Illinois has gone nearly an entire year without a state budget and what has been happening in Springfield since then.

        “Rauner’s turnaround agenda is a deep, philosophical disagreement that cannot be expected to be solved during a time of divided government,” Biss said. “These changes that the governor wants are pretty much impossible to imagine happening in any state that has a Republican governor but a big Democratic super majority.”

        Upon opening up the floor to attendees, questions were raised about other unpassed laws such as a house bill on gun violence, the clean jobs bill on renewable energy, pension reform, education and what the three state legislators at the meeting describe as the “fallacy” of the Democratic supermajority in Springfield.

        “Illinois is such a diverse state,” Fine said. “And what makes sense maybe in this part of the state is very hard to make a blanket law that will impact the southern part of the state.”

        Despite this, the worries of Illinois residents still stand. Sherman said he is concerned that if the state budget impasse does not get resolved soon, the state’s reputation will continue to go downhill, which he said will destroy the state’s economy in the long term.

        Biss said the people in Springfield need to acknowledge that there is a divided government and put that aside because the state legislature needs to exist “for the wellbeing of the residents of Illinois.”

        “Rauner makes me crazy, Madigan annoys me, and I wouldn’t be doing things the way they are if I were them,” Biss said. “But more important is for us as a society to work out these issues.”
