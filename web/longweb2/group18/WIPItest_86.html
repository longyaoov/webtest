    Assemblyman Jim Frazier bill calls for dyslexia screening at schools

    Share this:

    • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
    • Click to print (Opens in new window)
    By Richard Bammer | rbammer@thereporter.com | Vacaville Reporter
    UPDATED: August 29, 2018 at 12:00 AM PDT

    The genesis of a state Assembly bill may be as simple as talking over a cup of Joe.

    Consider the case of AB 1369 Special education: dyslexia.

    A bill introduced in February by Assemblyman Jim Frazier, it began to take shape some months ago during one of the Solano-area Democrat’s occasional coffee klatches with constituents, specifically one in Discovery Bay.

    “A mother approached me,” Frazier said Friday. “She was very frustrated with her school district. They were unwilling to help her (with her dyslexic child), so she went outside of the district to get help and paid out of her own pocket.

    “She was sharing with me that other parents don’t have the ability to do that … and their children are failing. It touched my soul.”

    The rest is recent history.

    Co-authored by Assembly members Kevin Mullin, D-South San Francisco, and Das Williams, D-Santa Barbara, the bill, as amended in April, would require school districts to screen all children in grades one to three, to determine if they are dyslexic or have other reading or writing problems, and to notify their parents if they do. Additionally, beginning with the 2016-17 school year, it would require local in-service training for school psychologists, regular and special education teachers, and require college and university teacher-training programs to provide lessons about how to recognize dyslexia and other major learning disabilities.

    It would also require the State Superintendent of Public Instruction to develop, by Jan. 1, 2017, program guidelines for dyslexia and other related learning disabilities to be used to help regular and special education teachers, and parents (as written) “to identify, assess, plan, provide, evaluate, and improve educational services to pupils, as specified.”

    As a mandated program, if approved by the Legislature and signed by Gov. Jerry Brown, it would require the state to reimburse school districts for certain costs deemed necessary.

    Frazier, who estimates that dyslexia affects one in five students, noted that the politically powerful California Teachers Association this week dropped its opposition to the bill. It is a development that improves its chances where it is now, in the Assembly Appropriations Committee. From there, if green-lighted, it goes to the Assembly floor for a vote, then to the state Senate Education and Appropriations committees before a vote on the Senate floor and, finally, on to the governor’s office for his signature.

    As Frazier mulled over the Discovery Bay mother’s story, he later learned from several adult friends how they managed to cope with dyslexia, a language processing disorder that can hinder learning, writing, spelling and speaking as well as social interactions and self-esteem.

    “They had frustration of not being able to manage it, of not being able to read,” he recalled. “They had not had any kind of help with their dyslexia. When I got back to the Capitol last year, we started doing research and working with Decoding Dyslexia” and eventually drafted and introduced the bill.

    Decoding Dyslexia, formed in 2011 by some New Jersey parents, is a group that supports it members in their efforts to get appropriate educational services for their children. A California group formed about two years ago and a Solano County group recently formed, largely spearheaded by Debbie and Sydney Salazar of Fairfield, whose son, Joshua, is dyslexic.

    Dyslexia afflicts an estimated 5 to 10 percent of all people. The condition is neurological and often passed down in families. It is not the result of poor instruction, and with the right kind of support and interventions, public or private, almost all dyslexic people can become good readers and writers, experts believe. Additionally, the list of famous people who had it — or have it — is astounding: Albert Einstein, Pablo Picasso and Muhammad Ali are among them.

    AB 1369 sailed through the Assembly Education Committee on a unanimous bipartisan vote, noted Frazier, who, upon some reflection, agreed that school teachers will be tasked to do even more in the classroom if the bill passes. However, the specialized training will permit them to have success —- and ensure success for their students as they grow academically, he believes.

    “I wish I could give them every tool in the tool box that they need,” he said of the teachers, adding that California’s large class sizes sometimes prevent teachers from devoting much-needed one-to-one attention with students struggling with learning disabilities.

    “You can’t teach them the way ordinary children are taught,” said Frazier. “You have to teach them the way they learn.”

    Frazier said it was his “impression” that massive state funding cutbacks during the Great Recession drastically crimped some services provided by Medi-Cal that, at one time, helped children with learning disabilities.

    “I just don’t want any parent disappointed and that their children are given every opportunity to learn,” he said. “And, again, the teachers are angels for what they are expected to do nowadays.”
