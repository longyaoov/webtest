Bill would make it easier to get drugs to reverse heroin overdoses

  • Published: Apr. 03, 2015, 8:31 p.m.
By
  • Amy Yurkanin | ayurkanin@al.com

JEFFCO HEROIN DEATHS

Heroin and Needle

(mark wragg)

The increasing number of deaths from heroin and other opiates has prompted a bill that would allow family and friends to get a drug that reverses overdoses and saves lives.

Medics in Jefferson County already carry naloxone hydrochloride, more commonly known as Narcan. The bill, HB 208, would allow dentists and physicians to prescribe the same drug to family members and friends of those who suffer from dangerous addictions. It would also provide training for law enforcement agencies that want to allow officers to carry the drug.

Narcan works by knocking opioids out of brain receptors, and it cannot be used to get high. The drug can be administered as an injection or a nasal spray. Narcan usually starts working within two to five minutes, according to the Harm Reduction Coalition, and is extremely effective in reversing overdoses.

The number of heroin overdose deaths more than doubled in Jefferson County in 2014 from the year before. The coroner's office confirmed 123 heroin deaths last year, compared to 58 the year before. Heroin isn't the only killer. Deaths from opioid pain killers have remained stubbornly high in Alabama, and the rate tripled between 1999 and 2013.

Representative Allen Treadaway (R-Morris), a Birmingham Police captain, sponsored the bill.

"I think it's needed to save lives," Treadaway said. "We've got a serious problem in our communities. One only has to look at the doubling of the overdose deaths in the last two years."

Jacque Meyer has two sons who have struggled with heroin addiction. One of them no longer uses the drug, but the other son still struggles with addiction. She has found him during an overdose, and she said the doctor told her he was within two hours of losing his life.

"He spent five days in the ICU," Meyer said.

Although she understands that access to the drug can provide lifesaving care, she has mixed feelings about making it more widely available.

"Whenever you are saving a life, especially if it was one of my son's, I would think it was absolutely fabulous," she said. "But how many times are you going to use Narcan to save somebody? I'm worried he would just think, 'If I'm around my mom, she'll save me.'"

Treadaway said he hopes the drug will save lives, and give addicts another chance to get clean. The drug does not affect people who aren't high on opioids, so if it is mistakenly given to someone who is diabetic or suffering a heart attack, it won't hurt them, Treadaway said.

For those who are using opioids, Narcan will cause immediate withdrawal.

"No addict wants to shoot this up," Treadaway said. "It creates a situation where you go into withdrawals."

That also concerns Meyer. Addicts who are given Narcan may wake up from the overdose upset and out-of-control, which is something family members should know if they are going to stock the drug.

Meyer does support the good Samaritan provisions of the law, which prevent prosecution for misdemeanor drug offenses and underage drinking for people who call 911 to report an overdose. Often, drug users will leave the scene of an overdose without reporting it out of fear of arrest, Meyer said.

Twenty-eight states have increased access to Narcan, according to the Network for Public Health Law. By 2010, these laws had resulted in 10,000 overdose reversals, according to the same group.

Rep. Treadaway introduced a similar bill last year that never made it out of committee. This year, his bill has more support from legislators and outside groups.

"It's not unusual with this addiction for folks to go through multiple rehabs before they get better," Treadaway said. "If there is a drug out there that can keep them alive until they find the cure that works, we want to have access to that."
