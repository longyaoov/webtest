Time goes funny when your eyes move
Friday, 16 November 2001 

Why does time sometimes appear to stand still?
Scientists say they can now explain why when you first turn your attention to a silently ticking clock, the second hand can appear to move slower than normal, and sometimes even stop.

Kielan Yarrow of the Institute of Neurology at University College, London, and colleagues report their research in this week's Nature.

They say the illusion is due to the way our brain makes sense of the tens of thousands of 'snapshots' our eyes take of the world every day.

When we're reading a line of text, for example, our eyes fix on a group of words and then flick to the next group of words.

Each flick of the eye is called a 'saccade', and we are thought to have about three saccadic eye movements per second during our waking life.

A question that has plagued scientists for years, however, is why we don't notice this saccade. Why is it that when you look from one eye to the other in a mirror, for example, you don't notice your eyes move?

Yarrow and colleagues found that the brain actually projects its perception of the second target backwards.

They proved this bending of time by asking people to look suddenly at a silently ticking clock, and asking them to describe what they saw.

The brain pretends to perceive the second target 50 milliseconds before the eyes actually start to saccade, they found.

This trick of the mind effectively suppresses the saccade and allows one target to be integrated seamlessly with the next.

During a saccadic eye movement, both time and space are compressed to smooth our view of the world. The bottom image shows the observer's impression of the Sydney Harbour Bridge just before a saccade: the brain pretends to perceive the second target before the eyes actually start to move.
"This occurs every time we move the eyes but it is only perceived when an external time reference alerts us to the phenomenon," they write.

"These data support ideas of conscious experience as an ongoing, often post hoc reconstruction emerging from multiple cognitive systems."

According to Professor John Ross of the University of Western Australia, the research has helped to address the question of what happens during saccadic eye movements.

"This is the first published experiment that proves time goes funny during a saccade," he told ABC Science Online.

Ross and colleagues presented similar findings at the 1997 European Conference on Visual Perception in Helsinki, Finland.

"We've found that when something happens just before, during, or after a saccade, it appears to happen at the end of the saccade," he said.

"Time is effectively compressed."
