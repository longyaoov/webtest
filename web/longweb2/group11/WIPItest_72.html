    For Maimed Veterans, Animal Tissue Offers Hope of Repair

    By The New York Times
    September 17, 2012 4:42 pm September 17, 2012 4:42 pm

    Thousands of veterans of the Iraq and Afghanistan wars have been maimed in explosions and have lost so much muscle from an arm or a leg that amputation is sometimes the best alternative. But in today’s paper, our colleague Henry Fountain tracks a new treatment still in development that helped one Marine regain much of his lost mobility — using material from a pig’s bladder.

    Sgt. Ron Strang lost most of his left thigh to a roadside bomb blast in Afghanistan in 2010 and he wondered if he would ever be able to walk normally again. But the sergeant was recruited for a new medical technique using a substance called extracellular matrix, the natural scaffolding that underlies all tissues and organs in both animals and humans. Doctors grafted a thin sheet of material, culled from a pig, on to remaining healthy muscle in Sergeant Strang’s thigh. It helped his thigh to repair and regrow.

    Mr. Fountain writes:

    Now he walks easily, can run on a treadmill and is thinking of a post-military career as a police officer. “If you know me, or know to look for it, you can see a slight limp,” he said. “But everybody else, they go, ‘I would never have guessed.’ ”

    The sergeant is one of the first subjects in what will eventually be an 80-patient trial to grow limb muscle. The research is financed by the Defense Department’s Office of Technology Transition, but will include civilians as well.
