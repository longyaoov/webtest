    Kubota’s field of dreams

    Share this:

    • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Reddit (Opens in new window)
    • Click to print (Opens in new window)
    By The Sun | thesun@dfmdev.com
    PUBLISHED: July 13, 2008 at 12:00 AM PDT

    In the early 1900s, some of the first Japanese settlers in the Los Angeles Basin began leasing farmland, which was originally owned by the Sepulveda family, on the Palos Verdes Peninsula.

    These Japanese settlers took advantage of the land’s rich soil and expanded their operations. Over the next decades, they cultivated fruits and vegetables throughout what would become the South Bay.

    As residential and industrial development swept through the area, farmland began to dwindle, but the influence of the Japanese did not.

    In 1972, Japan-based Kubota Corp., a tractor and heavy equipment manufacturer, established its U.S. headquarters, Kubota Tractor Corp., in Torrance.

    The Japanese, once responsible for the cultivation of several major crops in California, are now one of the nation’s leading providers of agricultural-based equipment.

    Kubota Tractor Corp., also known as KTC, specializes in high performance and durable heavy machinery, which includes tractors, construction equipment, consumer lawn and garden equipment, commercial turf products and utility vehicles.

    The company has more than 1,100 dealers in the United States, according to Greg Embury, vice president of sales and marketing.

    KTC’s core products, though, are compact and midsize tractors. It offers 65 different tractor models, Embury said.

    In the late 1960s, Kubota Corp. saw a demand for smaller tractors and farm equipment in the United States. Traditionally, Japanese farms were considerably smaller than those in America and, as a result, Japanese farmers tended to use smaller equipment.

    In 1969, Kubota Corp. introduced its first tractor to the U.S. market, the Kubota L200, which was used for small garden plots and small homeowner lawn and garden maintenance, Embury said.

    “What was happening in America was this demand, we felt, for small equipment for homeowner acreage owners – for small specialty gardeners,” Embury said. “So they brought their small equipment to America and found a niche market and it flourished.”

    The L200’s success led to the eventual founding of Kubota Tractor Corp. in 1972.

    Since then, the U.S headquarters’ broad product mix has allowed it to expand and participate in a variety of markets, from suburbia’s front lawns to the grape fields in Napa Valley to pulling dog sleds in Alaska.

    Retired people and hobby farmers represent KTC’s largest consumer base, Embury said. The second biggest customer is the commercial agriculture industry.

    In 2006, KTC sold its one millionth unit in the U.S. market. The following year, it celebrated 35 years in the United States.

    Unlike many of its competitors, whose final products often consist of parts made by other manufacturers, KTC is a fully integrated company.

    “We produce a larger majority of the finished product than any other manufacturer in the world,” Embury said. “We believe that gives us a competitive advantage in quality and fit between the pieces.”

    KTC gets roughly half of its products from its manufacturing and assembling facilities in Japan and the other half from its U.S. affiliates.

    KTC has four division offices, located in California, Georgia, Ohio and Texas. In addition, Kubota Manufacturing of America and Kubota Industrial Equipment are in Georgia; Kubota Engine America is in Illinois; and Kubota Credit Corp. and Kubota Insurance Corp. are in Torrance.

    Global sales for Kubota Corp are nearly $12 billion, with companies and affiliates similar to KTC located in more than 130 countries, including Canada, France, the United Kingdom, Germany and Australia. Embury said the North American business, its largest subsidiary, represents a “strong percentage” of the $12 billion in sales.

    Kubota Corp. has more than 22,000 employees worldwide, with 2,500 in North America. The company has 580 employees in KTC’s U.S. offices, which includes 167 at the Torrance-based office, according to Embury.

    Embury said KTC’s strength lies in its close relationship with Japan, pointing to the analytical and engineering strengths of Japanese culture and the marketing and creative strengths of American culture.

    “The power is in the multicultural collaboration,” Embury said. “I’m convinced of that.”

    In June, KTC took part in the National Safety Council’s annual celebration of National Safety Month to help spread awareness about tractor and equipment safety. The NSC is a non-profit organization dedicated to preventing accidental injuries and death.

    More than 700 farmers die in work-related incidents each year and another 120,000 agricultural workers suffer disabling injuries, according to the NSC. Many accidents happen during the use of farm-related equipment, such as the tractor. Nearly 250 people die nationwide from tractor-related incidents each year, according to the Western Farm Press, a magazine that covers the agricultural industry.

    Terence Criss, manager of customer satisfaction and head of KTC’s tractor safety information area, said one of the most common accidents that occurs when operating a tractor is a rollover. Operators can accidentally rollover a tractor when maneuvering through ditches, depressions, obstacles and hills.

    Criss says this problem can be deadly.

    Since 1985, KTC has installed rollover protection structures on all its tractors. The structures, which are basically a rollbar, provide a protective zone around the operator if a rollover occurs.

    If all tractors were equipped with seat belts and the protective structures, the NSC estimates, nearly 350 lives would be saved each year.

    Kubota also equips its tractors with cover shields around some of the moving parts, as well as an operator-present system that shuts off the tractor engine if the operator is no longer in the seat.

    Embury said KTC receives a clipping service of agricultural accidents that happen throughout the country.

    “When you read them, you start thinking about loved ones, the equipment that’s around them, trying to keep them safe, and it’s just education,” he said.

    For many Americans, though, tractor rides are considered an American pastime.

    “A customer once told me, `driving that tractor every minute I get has saved me a fortune in psychiatrist fees,”‘ Embury recalled.

    “The American dream is 10 acres and a horse. If you make and accomplish that dream, than you need a Kubota tractor to go with it.”
