    College job-seekers need to watch what they post, CSUF student finds

    Share this:

    • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Reddit (Opens in new window)
    • Click to print (Opens in new window)
    By Wendy Fawthrop | wfawthrop@scng.com | Orange County Register
    UPDATED: May 14, 2017 at 9:05 PM PDT

    This time of year many graduating students are sending off resumes and emails to attempt to find their first “real” job. But when hiring managers Google those candidates, what will they see?

    Han Tran set out to shed some light on how undergraduates manage their social media during a job search. To put it in 140 characters or less: Job-hunting students might need more guidance in what they post than we might think.

    Tran, a senior in business administration-marketing and finance, is one of two Mihaylo College of Business and Economics students to win this year’s Outstanding Student Scholarly and Creative Activities Award for an independent research project. She presented “Social Media Usage Among College Undergraduates: Is Job Searching a Priority?” at the Marketing Educators’ Association Conference last month in San Diego.

    “Han’s paper discusses a relevant and timely topic,” said her faculty mentor, Steven Chen, Mihaylo associate professor of marketing. “Her survey study proffers some significant results, which contributes to the social media literature.”

    Tran started with research confirming that, not surprisingly, today’s college students, who have been surrounded by technology their whole lives, are especially big users of social media sites to post updates, share videos and photos, maintain their profiles and send messages.

    Likewise, she cited research showing that many employers today regard social media as an inexpensive way to post job openings and look for prospective candidates. A 2015 study by the online recruiting platform Jobvite showed that 92 percent of recruiters used social media in the hiring process. Just over half of them said they have reconsidered candidates based on their social profiles, with 61 percent of those reconsiderations being negative.

    LinkedIn (92 percent), Facebook (66 percent) and Twitter (52 percent) were the social networks employers looked at most.

    Recruiters believe that social media lets them discover an applicant’s real character and whether it meshes with company culture, according to one study Tran cites. Individuals can customize their online profiles to showcase their hobbies, interests and preferences and upload pictures or videos that convey best their personalities at any time.

    Any concerns that it allows potential employers to glimpse private information that employment law prevents them from asking about – such as age, disability, gender reassignment, marital status and religious belief – appear to have been superseded by the practice’s expediency.

    The appropriate use of social media can distinguish a college student from competitors during the job application process. But beyond efforts by some universities to familiarize students with professional sites such as LinkedIn, the extent to which students capitalize on social media usage for career growth had been little studied, Tran said. One of the few studies showed students committing “posting paradox,” in which they intentionally shared inappropriate content online while acknowledging recruiters might see it.

    Using a 39-question survey, Tran aimed to get a handle on how undergraduates promote themselves on social media and monitor their profiles for career-oriented purposes. She invited 874 undergrads at Cal State Fullerton to take the survey, getting complete responses from 105. She also posted a link to the survey on student group Facebook pages at 23 other colleges nationwide, for a total of 197 complete surveys.

    She posited five theories she thought her research might prove. Two of them were borne out.

    The biggest findings:

    –Students who engaged in self-promotion exercised self-monitoring image control, which contradicted previous studies’  “posting paradox” findings.

    –Students who possessed high social media use were more likely to portray themselves positively via virtual networking sites. Common tactics included posting accomplishments, displaying superior skill sets and describing challenges to emphasize their capabilities.

    –While there was no significant difference in career engagement between males and females, male students were more likely to deploy assertive strategies to promote themselves via the social network. In particular, they were more active in updating their personal achievements. No significant difference between the genders regarding how they monitored their profiles was found, however.

    –Career engagement and social media affinity were consistent among all four classes, which contrasts with a study showing career involvement rising as students advance. But Tran found that seniors were more likely than juniors to exert control over the impression their online profile created, as Tran predicted. Seniors were likely to use LinkedIn, geared toward professionals, by a margin of 61 percent vs. 4.6 percent for freshmen.

    –No significant difference was found among ethnic groups in terms of engaging in career-oriented activities, social media preference, posting accomplishments and managing profiles, which was unexpected since previous studies show that students of Asian heritage typically put a greater emphasis on career development than other ethnicities, Tran said.

    –Students who proactively planned their career path or networked with professionals might not necessarily monitor their social media profiles. The study attributes that to a shortage of learning initiatives to educate students on the benefits of using social media for job seeking and on the importance of maintaining an appropriate online profile.

    “The more active the students are, the more attractive their social media profiles are to recruiters – the greater likelihood that they would be found by headhunters,” Tran writes. “At the same time, networking tactics and social media cleansing technique should also be incorporated to future initiatives. …

    “Early intervention methods targeting those students in specific areas can help improve their experience in college. Conversely, those students who rank high in all career-related aspects can be identified as potential peer mentors in helping and supporting other classmates in achieving great career development.”

    ***

    What — and what not — to post

    Job-seekers might want to do more than just hide those incriminating spring break photos, according to a 2014 study by the online recruiting platform Jobvite.

    While sexual-, drug- and alcohol-related posts were among the biggest red flags in job applicants’ social media profiles, mistakes in spelling and grammar were big turn-offs as well, as were posts about guns and those with profanity. Mentions of political affiliation left recruiters mostly neutral, while references to volunteerism and charity carried a big positive.

    The study warned about “zombie content.” Long-buried online activity can come back to hurt you well after you’ve forgotten about it, says Jobvite.

    What recruiters like to see, especially on LinkedIn: professional experience, length of professional tenure, industry-related posts, mutual connections, specific hard skills, cultural fit and examples of work.

    Don’t have a long job history? Balance it out with a review of the latest book about your chosen field or photos of you surfing for that activewear industry recruiter to see.

    Search for yourself online to find out what content potential employers will see, advises Jobvite.

    And take down the Cancun whipped cream swimsuit contest photo. Now.
