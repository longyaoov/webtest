Jimmy Dimora federal racketeering trial: Prosecutors quiz Cuyahoga County court official Tom Pokorny

  • Updated: Jan. 23, 2012, 5:45 p.m.
  • |Published: Jan. 23, 2012, 4:45 p.m.
By
  • Plain Dealer staff

AKRON, Ohio -- Longtime judge and former Cuyahoga County Common Pleas Court administrator Tom Pokorny took the stand this afternoon in the federal racketeering trial of Jimmy Dimora.

As court administrator, Pokorny was responsible in 2008 for setting the spending budget for court operations, including personnel.  He said he was asked to cut $500,000 from the budget that year and after meeting with Jim Ginley, the county's budget officer, decided that the money would be saved by taking it from the work-release program.

In short, that meant that Alternatives Agency, a Cleveland halfway house that helped people recently released from prisons, would have its budget slashed.

On Friday, witnesses told jurors that former county Commissioner Jimmy Dimora and other public officials accepted bribes in 2008 in exchange for restoring county funding to that agency.

They said county employee J. Kevin Kelley -- also a consultant for Alternatives -- set up a Las Vegas gambling trip meant to sway Dimora into giving a $250,000 contract to the agency.

In late 2007, Pokorny and his boss, Cuyahoga County Common Pleas Court Judge Nancy McDonnell, had recommended ending a two-year, $1.2 million contract for the agency's work-release program. Rather than accept the recommendation, Kelley and  Brian Schuman, who helped run the agency and testified last week, reached out to Dimora.

Marvin Fong, The Plain DealerFormer Cuyahoga County commissioner Jimmy Dimora, left, arrives with his attorney William Whitaker as members of the media chronicle his arrival.

Pokorny testified this morning that he had received a phone call from Dimora and two calls from then-county Auditor Frank Russo, asking him to consider spreading around the cuts, not just only Alternatives.

He also said he asked Dimora to call McDonnell directly -- and then got the OK from McDonnell to take half the budget cut from work-release (affecting Alternatives), but the other half would come from substance abuse programs.

McDonnell is also expected to testify in the trial.

FBI Special Agent Michael Massie had said last week that they also asked the commissioner to extract money from other county coffers. Dimora arranged a meeting of some of the leading county officials, heads of other halfway houses and Pokorny.

Today's testimony in the Dimora trial didn't begin until after 12:30 p.m. as U.S. District Judge Sara Lioi had given jurors an extra half-day off today following four full days of testimony and evidence last week.
