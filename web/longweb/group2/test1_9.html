Joe Pulizzi's Content Marketing World brings 1,750 digital marketers to Cleveland

  • Updated: Sep. 14, 2013, 2:20 a.m.
  • |Published: Sep. 14, 2013, 1:20 a.m.
By
  • Janet H. Cho, The Plain Dealer

C12SHATNERB_15362757.JPG

Actor William Shatner was the closing keynote speaker at the Content Marketing World conference, which drew more than 1,750 people from 40 nations to Cleveland, and contributed an estimated $3.9 million to the local economy over the past week.

(John Kuntz, The Plain Dealer)

CLEVELAND, Ohio -- Spending time with Joe Pulizzi, founder of the Content Marketing World conference, means being interrupted mid-sentence by attendees who hand you their iPads and ask you to take pictures of themselves with him.

Pulizzi is used to the attention. He's the public face of Content Marketing Institute, the upstart digital marketing company that brought more than 1,750 people from as far away as Australia and Thailand to the Cleveland Convention Center this week.

Pulizzi said his conference, which drew attendees from 40 nations, is the largest content marketing event on the planet. "And the best part? Seventy-five percent of them had never been to Cleveland before," he said.

"Cleveland's the center of the content industry," he said, shrugging. "I started using that term in 2001 before it became hip." He calls himself the "Godfather of Content Marketing."

Positively Cleveland, the region's convention and visitors bureau, estimates the economic impact from CMWorld at $3.9 million over the four-day conference.

That includes nearly 2,500 room nights at seven hotels, dining out at local restaurants, and other spending, but not the conference registration costs of up to $2,495 per person.

Content Marketing Institute is ranked 468 on Inc. magazine's list of America's 5,000 fastest-growing private companies for its 989 percent growth over the past three years. It reported revenues of $3.1 million in 2012. Its 14-member staff, all of whom work from home, balloons to 34 during CMWorld.

Dressed in his signature orange shirt, shoes and pocket square, Pulizzi presides over the convention like a celebrity, and even carries around a fine-point orange Sharpie to sign autographs.

Many of those who approach him do so toting copies of his latest book, "Epic Content Marketing: How to Tell a Different Story, Break Through the Clutter and Win More Customers by Marketing Less."

Of the attendees, "10 percent of the people have the word 'content' in their titles. The others are marketing directors, social media directors, or people who in some way, shape or form are responsible for content," he said.

They're the kind of people who, when actor William Shatner took the CMWorld stage on Wednesday night, they sent out more than 1,212 tweets an hour about it.

So what exactly is content marketing?

Vijay Palaparty, a 1998 graduate of Westlake High School who now works as director of communications and finance writer for NACHA, the Electronic Payments Association in Herndon, Va., was a first-time attendee.

"We all have stories to tell, we all have content. But how do you use that in effective ways to connect with the right people?" he asked.

Kate Eidam, a business lead at Akhia Public Relations in Hudson, put it this way: "Consumers are accustomed to ignoring advertising. It's often just background noise. At the same time, they demand more from brands in terms of relevant information, transparency and responsiveness."

"This leaves a gap, which is, in turn, an opportunity for marketers to reach customers with authentic, relevant, helpful and engaging information and conversations," she said. "Content marketing is at the core of filling this gap, as well as helping brands increase revenue, conversion and awareness."

"It's the best conference I've been to in years," she added. "The idea-gathering, creation, curation and exchange has been unprecedented. It's one of those conferences you leave feeling re-energized and more excited than ever about the work you do every day."

"Who knew Cleveland was so nice?"

This is exactly the kind of tech-savvy crowd Pulizzi envisioned when he and his employees hosted the first Content Marketing World gathering here in 2011.

They reserved a room at the Renaissance Cleveland Hotel hoping 100 to 150 people might show up. "We ended up getting 660, and took over the Renaissance," Pulizzi said.

The next year, they were forced to move the conference to Columbus. "As a Cleveland guy, I wanted it in Cleveland, but there was no venue that could hold us," he said. "It was kind of depressing when you think about it, for a city of our size."

So when the $465 million Cleveland Convention Center announced it was opening last month, CMWorld was among the first to jump aboard.

"What we're hearing from attendees is 'It's so clean!' 'Who knew Cleveland was this nice?' 'There's so much history here' 'It's so easy to get around'" Pulizzi said. "Of course, we only showed them the good stuff," he joked.

CMWorld is the second-largest event of visitors, after the 2013 National Senior Games, that the Convention Center has hosted so far, said Dave Johnson, director of public relations for the Convention Center and the Global Center for Health Innovation.

"This is a significant event because it illustrates the direct economic impact a convention center can have on a city," by bringing back a conference it wouldn't have been able to attract before, Johnson said. "Any time you bring people in from outside the seven-county region, they're spending money and leaving their money behind."

It also stretched the convention center's capacity to provide audiovisual, WiFi, food and beverage, custodial and other services required for a group of its size, he said. The fact that there were no major hiccups bodes well for future gatherings.

"As of today, there are 155 events scheduled here through the end of calendar year 2014, with 213,000 attendees and 89,000 group hotel room nights," Johnson said.

Among them is the 2014 Content Marketing World conference, from Sept. 8-10.

Pulizzi said his group is only a small piece of what could be a much bigger comeback for Cleveland. "We have so many amazing corporations, and all of them have events: Lincoln Electric, Sherwin-Williams, Eaton, the law firms.

"Why are they not bringing their events here?" he asked. "That's what I'd like to know."
