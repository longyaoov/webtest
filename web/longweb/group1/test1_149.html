    By Chicago Tribune
    UPDATED: August 11, 2021 at 1:23 AM CDT

    Think of a deeper order of beauty,

    A kind of magnificence whose essence

    Lies in estrangement, the anxiety

    Of the unrecognized . . .

    — John Koethe,

    “The Secret Amplitude”

    Some days, when the early-evening light seemed to descend upon the Chicago skyline like folds of pastel drapery, Scott Fortino’s eyes would rise from whatever manner of human misfortune he was confronting and he would know a secret joy.

    Everything around him argued against such joy: the tall, grim buildings, the blank landscape that pooled around those buildings like dried-up moats, the crumpled bags emblazoned with the names of fast-food emporiums, the scent of futility.

    But to Fortino, 45, a Chicago police officer, the light changed everything. Even here in the Cabrini-Green public housing project, a place that many Chicagoans regard as “dangerous, with nightmarish criminality,” as Fortino puts it, he felt a steadying happiness.

    He loved the light in the sky. He loved the way the buildings muscled their way into that sky. And he loved walking through the buildings, seeing the way the light from the windows shed a casual radiance on ordinary objects such as doors and walls.

    “I can walk into a housing project and be transported by the quality of the light,” Fortino said. “That’s what got me through seeing the other things — the things you associate with public housing: the way the light falls on the buildings.”

    When it came time for him to undertake a project for his graduate thesis in photography at the University of Illinois at Chicago, then, Fortino knew his destination: Cabrini, where he had walked a beat since 1988.

    Starting last summer, Fortino began to take pictures of the front doors of Cabrini apartments.

    “Doors have a psychological weight to them,” he said in the quiet, understated way he talks about his work. “It just seemed to make sense to me.”

    Initially, he photographed doors here and there, when he found himself intrigued by a particular color or a smattering of graffiti. That approach troubled him, however; he wanted the project to be more systematic, to record the range of Cabrini thresholds, not simply his own tastes and whims.

    So he selected a single building — 911 N. Hudson St. — and took pictures of all 48 doors in the six-story structure. “I chose it because it was manageable,” Fortino said. “Some of the buildings are 19 stories, with 20 apartments per floor. That was too much.”

    He and a friend, Tom Nowak, who helped with the lighting, were careful to treat the doors identically, “to maintain a consistent viewpoint,” Fortino said. He set up his camera and tripod the same distance from each entryway.

    He asked residents for permission to take the pictures; none seemed to care one way or another. “We initiated conversations with women going in and out of the apartments. Instead of being curious about us, mostly they wanted to know why things weren’t getting fixed like they ought to be.”

    Fortino knows that his access to Cabrini — an area riddled with crime and gang violence — is enabled by his profession.

    “When I’m there, it is known that I am the police. We use that knowledge as protection,” he said. “I have a radio with me. I am armed. Tom wears a bulletproof vest.” Without those preparations, “We would be prey, rather than on an equal footing.”

    When Fortino recently displayed the photographs for a committee of UIC professors, a requirement for graduate students in the School of Art & Design, the reaction was intense. Many appreciated Fortino’s careful archiving of objects not often viewed by anyone but the very poor; others felt Fortino had somehow insulted Cabrini residents, invading their privacy by presenting their front doors in such a stark, blunt manner.

    One thing was clear, however: Fortino’s work left no one unmoved. And if provoking a reaction is one of the purposes of art, if the point is to shake people out of their numbed neutrality, then the photographs are undeniably, if disturbingly, successful.

    Also, rather unexpectedly, the photographs are suffused with beauty.

    “Beauty” is not a word Fortino or many other serious photographers would employ. It is an unfashionable word because, as Fortino explained, “It’s hierarchical. If something is beautiful, that means something else is ugly, and that’s a value judgment.”

    An amateur onlooker, however, can say it out loud without fear of rebuke: The doors, in their color and variety, are beautiful in a scruffy, utilitarian way. They are as complicated as faces; like faces, they both hide and reveal, keep in and keep out. To some, a closed door may be an unfinished sentence or a held breath. In the next moment, anything can happen.

    The collection of Cabrini doors, moreover, subtly recalls the well-known poster series celebrating doors in places such as Dublin, doors with fancy carving and stained glass. A door is a door is a door — except that it isn’t.

    Nowak, who teaches photography part time at Columbia College, said he initially wondered about the point of Fortino’s project. “I thought, `Doors?’ and I said, `Scott, can’t you find something else?’ But as we walked through the building, I saw that the doors had character. They weren’t all the same, by any means. Each door was different.”

    Esther Parada, a UIC photography professor for whose class Fortino undertook his “doorway project,” as he calls it, said the discomfort aroused by the photographs may originate in concerns among documentary photographers about the objectification of poverty and other conditions.

    “Since the late 1970s, there has been a lot more attention to the power dynamics in photography: Who’s looking at whom and through what lens? It’s all about the manipulation of framing and point of view.”

    In earlier times, people looked at photographs as simple truth. As Susan Sontag phrased it in “On Photography,” her 1977 book, “Photographs furnish evidence. A photograph passes for incontrovertible proof that a given thing happened.” Words could be slanted, but pictures didn’t lie: “What is written about a person or event is frankly an interpretation, as are handmade visual statements, like paintings and drawings. Photographed images do not seem to be statements of the world as much as pieces of it, miniatures of reality.”

    Now, however, virtually everyone concedes that photographs are no more truthful than anything else, that they represent a prejudiced point of view just as do words. The concern, therefore, is that by choosing certain objects or people of which to make pictures, the photographer exerts power over the subjects.

    In other words, while we have Fortino’s view of the Cabrini doors, we do not — because of a variety of factors — have the Cabrini residents’ view of Fortino’s door or their own doors. Whether we think of it consciously or not, photographs are transactions, representing power relationships. Race adds another complication: Fortino is white, Cabrini predominantly black; he is an outsider looking at a world not his own.

    Cabrini spokeswoman Carol Steele, while unfamiliar with Fortino’s project, said she is glad someone saw beyond what she regards as the buildings’ ill-kept exteriors — but she wishes he had gone past the individual front doors as well.

    “The media is always taking pictures of the outsides of the buildings,” said Steele, president of the Cabrini Rowhouse Management Council. “They don’t go inside. People don’t realize that (Cabrini residents) take care of their places too. They live just like everybody else.”

    Fortino, for his part, is unfazed by questions and reservations. In many ways, the project represents the point at which his two worlds — law enforcement and graduate school — intersect, and so he anticipated a few ragged edges and mismatched surfaces.

    “For a long time, I felt the two worlds would never meet,” he recalled. “Among my friends in the police department, I was considered very liberal, because I was going to art school. Among my friends in the art world, I was very conservative, because I was the police.

    “Now, one seems to inform the other. My police life has become a large part of my art life.”

    Born and raised on Chicago’s Southwest Side, Fortino remembers hearing his father and uncle talk about the public housing structures that were pounded in like spikes against the city’s skyline. “They were talked about in hushed tones, as dangerous places. You hear those things when you’re a kid and there’s a sense of wonder, of curiosity.”

    Just before graduation from Columbia College, Fortino joined the police department. He enrolled in graduate photography courses at UIC, but police work gradually absorbed him and he dropped out in 1981.

    “I was wrapped up in the lifestyle of being the police,” he said. “The thesis just couldn’t get done.”

    Returning to UIC last year, he found that the two worlds seemed less antithetical. “Things happen for a reason,” said Fortino, who will remain with the police force but hopes to display his photographs in a gallery. “This is right for me now.”

    Fortino is no longer assigned to Cabrini. When he goes there to take photographs, though, the residents remember him. And he remembers them, as he admires the complicated light surrounding their homes.

    “The light — especially at this time of year — is very, very nice. I could look at it all day.”

    As Fortino knows, he may not be looking much longer at the light falling on Cabrini. As part of its $1 billion project to demolish some 19,000 public housing units and resettle the city’s poor in the next decade, the Chicago Housing Authority has torn down two of Cabrini’s 24 buildings; six more are scheduled to fall. While the building in which Fortino has been working is not on the list at present, according to a CHA spokesperson the demolition project will alter the Cabrini landscape irrevocably.

    “I wanted to make a record, a way to preserve this,” Fortino said. “It comes down to an appreciation for what’s around you.”
